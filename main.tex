\begin{abstract}
Dynamic Tanh (DyT) is a learnable element-wise replacement for transformer normalization layers that has been validated when trained from initialization. We study a different regime: replacing LayerNorm with DyT inside a LayerNorm-pretrained vision encoder, then fine-tuning on a downstream task. We test this post-hoc replacement on two computer vision tasks: image classification (ViT-B/16 fine-tuned on CIFAR-100) and semantic segmentation (SegFormer-B0 fine-tuned on a 2000-image ADE20K subset). We compare the paper-default DyT initialization ($\gamma{=}\mathbf{1}$, $\beta{=}\mathbf{0}$, $\alpha_0{=}0.5$) with an affine-preserving adaptation that copies the pretrained LayerNorm $\gamma, \beta$ into DyT at replacement time. On CIFAR-100, affine preservation improves post-hoc DyT from 4.50\% to 48.32\% top-1, still well below the 92.91\% LayerNorm baseline. On the ADE20K subset, both DyT variants fail near chance (0.0099 and 0.0138 mIoU, $3$--$4\times$ the $\approx 0.0033$ chance baseline), while LayerNorm reaches 0.0765, roughly $23\times$ chance. Diagnostics identify two distinct failure modes: tanh saturation in ViT default that affine init removes, and a loss of per-token mean-and-variance normalization in SegFormer that no init we tried recovers. Full post-hoc DyT replacement is therefore not a safe drop-in substitute for pretrained vision encoders.
\end{abstract}
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Transformer-based models have become standard components of modern computer vision systems, including image classification, semantic segmentation, and other dense prediction tasks~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit,xie2021segformer}. These models commonly inherit the same transformer block structure: attention, feed-forward layers, residual connections, and normalization~\cite{vaswani2017attention}. LayerNorm is especially central to this design, and it is often treated as a default part of the transformer stack rather than as a component whose necessity should be tested for each visual task.

Recent work challenges this assumption. Zhu et al. propose Dynamic Tanh (DyT), a learnable element-wise transformation that replaces transformer normalization layers without computing sample-wise means or variances~\cite{zhu2025dyt}. DyT is introduced as a drop-in replacement for LayerNorm or RMSNorm, and the authors validate it across settings ranging from recognition to generation, supervised to self-supervised learning, and computer vision to language models~\cite{zhu2025dyt}. This suggests that some of LayerNorm's practical role may be reproduced by a simpler bounded nonlinearity.

The DyT paper reports broad transformer results, but in every reported experiment DyT is in the network from the start of training~\cite{zhu2025dyt}. It does not test the post-hoc workflow common in applied computer vision: start from a public LayerNorm-pretrained backbone, replace its LayerNorm modules, and fine-tune on a downstream task. This kind of normalization surgery is plausible. Heimersheim shows that LayerNorm can be removed from GPT-2 by fine-tuning~\cite{heimersheim2024remove}. No analogous test exists for DyT in a pretrained vision transformer.

This regime matters because most computer vision applications do not train from scratch. The dominant practice is to download a public pretrained checkpoint and fine-tune it on a downstream task~\cite{kornblith2019better,kolesnikov2020bit}. A normalization replacement that only works under from-scratch training therefore reaches a much narrower audience than one that survives this workflow. Additionally, semantic segmentation remains untested by the DyT paper. Semantic segmentation is a dense prediction task that assigns a class label to each pixel without distinguishing separate instances of the same class~\cite{long2015fcn,torralba2024foundations}. Ranftl et al. observe that feature resolution and granularity, which can be discarded in classification, are critical for dense prediction~\cite{ranftl2021dpt}. Evaluating DyT in both classification and segmentation therefore widens the test beyond image-level recognition.

This work evaluates whether DyT can replace LayerNorm in this fine-tune-time regime, in two pretrained vision encoders: ViT-B/16~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit} fine-tuned on CIFAR-100~\cite{krizhevsky2009cifar100}, and SegFormer-B0~\cite{xie2021segformer} fine-tuned on an ADE20K subset~\cite{zhou2017ade20k}. In each setting we compare three variants: the LayerNorm baseline, DyT default following Algorithm~1 of Zhu et al.~\cite{zhu2025dyt}, and a single proposed adaptation that copies the pretrained LayerNorm affine parameters into DyT's $\gamma$ and $\beta$ at the moment of replacement. Evaluation pairs the downstream metric (top-1 accuracy or mIoU) with convergence curves, per-layer trained $\alpha$, and DyT activation saturation.

This work makes three main contributions. First, it presents the first evaluation of post-hoc DyT replacement in pretrained vision encoders, covering both image classification (ViT-B/16 on CIFAR-100) and semantic segmentation (SegFormer-B0 on an ADE20K subset). Second, it introduces an affine-preserving DyT initialization that copies the pretrained LayerNorm $\gamma, \beta$ into DyT, which recovers about half of the post-hoc classification gap relative to the paper-default initialization but does not improve segmentation. Third, it identifies two distinct failure modes through per-layer $\alpha$ and saturation diagnostics: $\tanh$ saturation in ViT default that affine init removes, and a loss of per-token mean-and-variance normalization in SegFormer that no tested init recovers.



\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Transformer Backbones for Vision}

Vision Transformers showed that pure transformer encoders can match convolutional networks on image classification when pretrained at sufficient scale~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit}. Subsequent work adapted transformer architectures for pixel-level prediction: SegFormer pairs a hierarchical transformer encoder with a lightweight MLP decoder for efficient semantic segmentation~\cite{xie2021segformer}, and Mask2Former uses masked attention as a unified architecture for semantic, instance, and panoptic segmentation~\cite{cheng2022mask2former}. All of these models retain LayerNorm as a default in their transformer components. This work adopts ViT-B/16 and SegFormer-B0 as testbeds; neither has been evaluated with DyT.

\subsection{Normalization in Transformers}

LayerNorm normalizes hidden features within each example, removing the dependence on batch statistics used by earlier methods such as Batch Normalization~\cite{ioffe2015batchnorm,ba2016layernorm}. It became a standard component of transformer blocks, where it is used with residual connections, attention layers, and feed-forward sublayers~\cite{vaswani2017attention}. Later work shows that the details of normalization matter. RMSNorm removes mean-centering while retaining re-scaling, suggesting that not all parts of LayerNorm are equally necessary~\cite{zhang2019rmsnorm}. Analyses of Pre-LN and Post-LN transformers show that normalization placement affects gradient behavior and training stability~\cite{xiong2020layernorm}. These studies treat normalization as a design choice rather than a fixed component. Each modifies normalization rather than removing or replacing it outright.

\subsection{Dynamic Tanh and Normalization-Free Transformers}

Dynamic Tanh replaces transformer normalization with a learnable bounded nonlinearity~\cite{zhu2025dyt}. Given an input $x$, DyT applies
\begin{equation}
    \dyt(x) = \gamma \odot \tanh(\alpha x) + \beta,
    \label{eq:dyt}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is a learnable scalar and $\gamma, \beta$ are learnable affine parameters. Unlike LayerNorm, DyT does not compute a sample-wise mean or variance. Instead, it uses a bounded element-wise nonlinearity followed by learned scaling and shifting.

The motivation for DyT is that trained LayerNorm modules in transformers often exhibit tanh-like input-output behavior~\cite{zhu2025dyt}. Zhu et al.\ show that replacing normalization layers with DyT can match or exceed normalized counterparts across several transformer settings, challenging the assumption that normalization is indispensable. Heimersheim shows that LayerNorm can be removed from a pretrained GPT-2 by fine-tuning, providing one instance of post-pretraining normalization surgery in the language-model setting~\cite{heimersheim2024remove}. This work adopts the DyT formulation directly to test the analogous question in pretrained vision transformer encoders.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

The methodology is a controlled post-hoc intervention on pretrained models. Rather than training a DyT transformer from initialization, we start from a LayerNorm-pretrained checkpoint, replace the relevant LayerNorm modules, and fine-tune the modified model on the same downstream data as the unmodified baseline. This setup directly tests the practitioner-facing question from the introduction: whether DyT can be inserted into a model that was not originally trained with DyT. The comparison is intentionally narrow. Within each task, the LayerNorm baseline and both DyT variants share the same data, optimizer, training length, and evaluation procedure, so performance differences can be attributed primarily to the normalization replacement and DyT initialization. \Cref{fig:method} summarizes the workflow.

\subsection{Post-hoc replacement and backbones}
\label{subsec:posthoc}

Zhu et al. validate DyT only when it is present from the start of training or pretraining; they do not swap DyT into an existing LayerNorm checkpoint~\cite{zhu2025dyt}. We isolate this post-hoc setting: each variant starts from a public pretrained checkpoint, performs the LayerNorm-to-DyT replacement before the first optimizer step, and then fine-tunes all parameters on the downstream task. This tests whether downstream fine-tuning can absorb the changed normalization map while the rest of the pretrained weights start unchanged.

We use ViT-B/16~\cite{dosovitskiy2021vit} for classification, fine-tuned on CIFAR-100~\cite{krizhevsky2009cifar100}, loaded from \texttt{google/vit-base-patch16-224} through HuggingFace Transformers~\cite{wolf2020transformers}. CIFAR-100 is a feasible classification anchor under our single-GPU class-project budget; full ImageNet-1K from-init training, which Zhu et al. report in their Table~1, is infeasible in this regime, and an ImageNet-100 subset would add substantial setup overhead for marginal scientific gain over CIFAR-100 in a within-project comparative study. ViT-B/16 itself is the same architecture Zhu et al. report in their Table~1, which keeps the model axis aligned with the original DyT evaluation. We do not test DeiT (same architecture as ViT, different pretraining recipe) or ConvNeXt (CNN, out of scope for LN-in-transformer replacement).

For segmentation we use SegFormer-B0~\cite{xie2021segformer} with the MiT-B0 encoder, loaded from \texttt{nvidia/mit-b0}, fine-tuned on a fixed-seed ADE20K~\cite{zhou2017ade20k} subset that preserves ADE20K's 150-class semantic diversity at one-tenth the training data. Full ADE20K with the official 160k-iteration SegFormer recipe is outside budget~\cite{xie2021segformer,mmseg2020}. PASCAL VOC trades semantic diversity for speed, and Cityscapes' high-resolution urban scenes amplify compute cost without adding scope to a normalization study.

The replacement walks \texttt{model.named\_modules()} and substitutes every \texttt{nn.LayerNorm} under a task-specific prefix: \texttt{vit.} for ViT and \texttt{segformer.encoder.} for SegFormer. The ViT prefix matches 25 LayerNorms: 24 inside the transformer blocks plus the final pre-classifier \texttt{vit.layernorm}. The SegFormer prefix matches 30 LayerNorms across the MiT-B0 encoder: patch embeddings, transformer blocks, stage outputs, and spatial-reduction modules. We do not exclude any LayerNorm by choice: the ViT classifier head is a single \texttt{Linear} with no normalization, and SegFormer's all-MLP decode head uses BatchNorm rather than LayerNorm, so the encoder prefix already covers every LayerNorm in either model~\cite{xie2021segformer}.

\subsection{LayerNorm and Dynamic Tanh}
\label{subsec:dyt}

For an input feature vector $x \in \mathbb{R}^d$, LayerNorm normalizes along the channel dimension using the sample's mean and variance:
\begin{equation}
    \lnorm(x) = \gamma \odot \frac{x - \mu(x)}{\sqrt{\sigma^2(x) + \epsilon}} + \beta.
    \label{eq:ln}
\end{equation}
The constant $\epsilon$ prevents division by zero when a layer's input has near-zero variance. We do not set $\epsilon$ ourselves. Each checkpoint ships its own value, and we inherit it: $10^{-12}$ for \texttt{google/vit-base-patch16-224}, $10^{-6}$ for \texttt{nvidia/mit-b0}. DyT (\cref{eq:dyt}) replaces this sample-wise normalization, with the per-channel $\gamma, \beta$ taking the same shape as in \cref{eq:ln} and no $\epsilon$ because no division occurs.

\subsection{Two initializations: DyT default and DyT affine}
\label{subsec:init}

Algorithm~1 of Zhu et al. initializes DyT with $\gamma = \mathbf{1}$, $\beta = \mathbf{0}$, and $\alpha = 0.5$~\cite{zhu2025dyt}. In a post-hoc checkpoint, downstream weights were trained against the LayerNorm affine output $\gamma_{\mathrm{LN}}, \beta_{\mathrm{LN}}$. Resetting those parameters changes both the normalization map and its learned affine range.

We therefore study two DyT initializations in the post-hoc regime:
\begin{itemize}
    \item DyT default: $\gamma = \mathbf{1}$, $\beta = \mathbf{0}$, $\alpha = 0.5$, exactly as Algorithm~1. Zhu et al. report from-init training to be insensitive to $\alpha_0$ in $[0.5, 1.2]$ (their Figure~9), so we hold $\alpha_0 = 0.5$ in this paper rather than sweep it.
    \item DyT affine: $\gamma \leftarrow \gamma_{\mathrm{LN}}$, $\beta \leftarrow \beta_{\mathrm{LN}}$, $\alpha = 0.5$. The LayerNorm \texttt{weight} and \texttt{bias} tensors are copied into DyT's $\gamma$ and $\beta$; $\alpha$ has no LayerNorm analog and stays at the default.
\end{itemize}

These two DyT variants are compared against an unmodified LayerNorm baseline on each of the two tasks, for six runs total.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{subsec:dataset}

\noindent Classification. CIFAR-100~\cite{krizhevsky2009cifar100} is loaded from \texttt{uoft-cs/cifar100} through HuggingFace Datasets~\cite{lhoest2021datasets}.

\noindent Segmentation. ADE20K~\cite{zhou2017ade20k} is loaded from the \texttt{merve/scene\_parse\_150} HuggingFace mirror through HuggingFace Datasets~\cite{lhoest2021datasets}. The subset uses 2000 train images and 500 validation images, drawn at fixed seed; all segmentation variants share the same indices.

\subsection{Training setup}
\label{subsec:training}

The training setup is designed for controlled within-task comparison rather than for a maximally tuned benchmark. Each task is run in three configurations: a LayerNorm baseline, DyT default, and DyT affine. Within a task, these configurations use the same seed (42), data subset, optimizer, learning-rate decay rule, augmentation policy, and evaluation cadence. The intended differences are only the normalization module and, for DyT, the initialization.

The per-task fine-tuning recipes are listed in \cref{tab:protocol}. We use one learning rate per task and do not tune it separately for each configuration, so the comparison isolates the normalization replacement rather than per-configuration hyperparameter search. These are shortened fine-tuning runs for within-task comparison, not reproductions of the from-initialization ImageNet experiments in the original DyT evaluation~\cite{zhu2025dyt}.

Train loaders shuffle to avoid a fixed data order and drop the final incomplete batch so every optimizer step has the same batch size. At validation time, classification uses a fixed center crop, while segmentation uses a fixed resize/crop and upsamples logits to label resolution before scoring. Pixels labeled 255 are ignored.

All runs use Colab Pro on a single NVIDIA A100-SXM4-80GB with PyTorch~\cite{paszke2019pytorch} automatic mixed precision.

To support reproducibility, the accompanying notebook records the checkpoint identifiers, replacement prefixes, dataset subset sizes, fixed seed, and run configurations used to generate the tables and figures. Each completed run also saves its configuration, training log, and summary metrics.\footnote{Code and run logs: \url{https://github.com/zhizhen-kyle-luo/6s058-final-project}} This is important for this project because the main claim is a negative comparison: without the shared run settings and logs, a DyT failure could be mistaken for an implementation or tuning artifact.

\subsection{Evaluation metrics and diagnostics}
\label{subsec:metrics}

For classification, we use top-1 accuracy for single-label image classification~\cite{torralba2024foundations}. Let $y_i$ be the ground-truth class for validation image $i$ and let $s_{i,c}$ be the model score for class $c$. The reported percentage is $100 \times \mathrm{Top}\mbox{-}1$, where
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{Top}\mbox{-}1 =
    \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}
    \mathbf{1}\!\left[\arg\max_c s_{i,c} = y_i\right].
    \label{eq:top1}
\end{equation}
For segmentation, we use the standard mean-IU/mIoU metric for semantic segmentation~\cite{long2015fcn}. For class $c$, with true positives $\mathrm{TP}_c$, false positives $\mathrm{FP}_c$, and false negatives $\mathrm{FN}_c$ accumulated over the validation set,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \mathrm{IoU}_c &=
    \frac{\mathrm{TP}_c}{\mathrm{TP}_c + \mathrm{FP}_c + \mathrm{FN}_c},\\
    \miou &= \frac{1}{C}\sum_{c=1}^{C}\mathrm{IoU}_c .
\end{aligned}
    \label{eq:miou}
\end{equation}
Here $C=150$ for ADE20K, and pixels labeled 255, which mark ADE20K's unannotated regions, are excluded from both the loss and the IoU counts. We compute the counts with a streaming confusion matrix. Training loss is logged every 50 iterations and validation metrics every 500 iterations. For DyT runs we also record:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the final learned $\alpha$ scalar for each DyT layer;
    \item the fraction of recorded activations satisfying $|\tanh(\alpha x)| > 0.95$, averaged per layer.
\end{itemize}

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

The results answer three questions. First, full post-hoc DyT replacement does not preserve the LayerNorm baseline. Second, copying the pretrained LayerNorm affine parameters helps classification but not segmentation. Third, the diagnostics do not point to a single failure mode across tasks. All numbers are from a single seed per configuration; the LayerNorm-versus-DyT gaps dwarf plausible seed variance, but the small within-DyT differences on segmentation should be read with this caveat.

\Cref{fig:curves} shows that the endpoint gaps reflect different training dynamics. In classification, the LayerNorm model completes most of its fine-tuning by the first validation checkpoint, while DyT default stays near the random cross-entropy scale for CIFAR-100 ($\log 100 \approx 4.61$) and barely improves in top-1. Affine preservation changes the trajectory: validation accuracy rises throughout training, indicating that copying $\gamma$ and $\beta$ makes the post-hoc ViT optimization problem less stuck, although still far from the LayerNorm baseline.

Segmentation behaves differently. The LayerNorm run improves steadily in mIoU over the full schedule, whereas both DyT variants make only small early gains and remain near chance. Their training losses still move and fluctuate, so the failure is not a clean numerical divergence. Instead, the models appear able to reduce pixelwise cross-entropy somewhat without learning segmentations that improve class-level overlap. This mismatch between loss and mIoU is consistent with a dense-prediction failure rather than a simple need for more iterations.

\includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{figures/segmentation_saturation.pdf}
\caption{Per-layer DyT saturation fraction, time-averaged over recorded forward passes during training and evaluation as the fraction of activations satisfying $|\tanh(\alpha x)| > 0.95$. Top: classification (ViT-B/16). Bottom: segmentation (SegFormer-B0). High values indicate feature-range collapse; low values indicate the layer operates near the linear regime of $\tanh$.}
\label{fig:saturation}
\end{figure*}

\Cref{fig:alpha} shows an asymmetry between the two ViT DyT variants. DyT default $\alpha$ stays nearly frozen at the initialization $\alpha_0=0.5$ across all 25 layers, whereas DyT affine $\alpha$ drifts above 0.5 in the early and middle blocks and spikes at the final pre-classifier layer. DyT default therefore did not learn to adjust $\alpha$, consistent with its near-zero classification progress; the affine variant trained $\alpha$ in addition to $\gamma$ and $\beta$. Segmentation $\alpha$ drifts in both variants (per-run means 0.520--0.531), so the segmentation failure is not a case of $\alpha$ failing to update.

Saturation in \cref{fig:saturation} explains the ViT contrast. DyT default saturation grows with depth and peaks near 0.96 at the final pre-classifier layer (mean 0.76, 21 of 25 layers above 0.5), while DyT affine is uniformly low (mean 0.17, only 4 of 25 layers above 0.5). Copying $\gamma, \beta$ therefore keeps every ViT layer in $\tanh$'s near-linear regime, consistent with the classification recovery. SegFormer behaves differently. Both DyT variants run at very low saturation (means 0.007 and 0.060), so $|\alpha x|$ is small for nearly all activations; first-order Taylor expansion of $\tanh$ around 0 gives $\tanh(\alpha x) \approx \alpha x$, so DyT collapses to a per-channel affine map:
\begin{equation}
    \dyt(x) \;\approx\; \gamma \odot (\alpha x) + \beta \;=\; (\alpha \gamma) \odot x + \beta .
    \label{eq:dyt-linear}
\end{equation}
That this affine-only configuration still fails to learn segmentation rules out $\tanh$ clipping as the SegFormer failure mode. The remaining structural difference between DyT-affine and LayerNorm in this regime is per-sample mean-centering and variance-scaling.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The post-hoc and from-initialization regimes are genuinely different problems. Downstream layers in a pretrained transformer were optimized against LayerNorm's per-token mean-centered and variance-scaled features; swapping LayerNorm for DyT replaces this per-token adjustment with a fixed channel-wise affine, so the downstream weights begin fine-tuning from inputs whose first and second moments no longer match what they were trained on, and the present results show that the budgets we tested do not close that mismatch. The classification-versus-segmentation asymmetry is consistent with Ranftl et al.'s observation that dense prediction depends on feature granularity that classification can discard~\cite{ranftl2021dpt}. Copying $\gamma, \beta$ is enough to recover a useful image-level representation but not enough to recover the spatially detailed features SegFormer needs. Concretely, LayerNorm's per-token mean-centering and variance-scaling adapts to each token's mean and variance over the channel dimension, while DyT's channel-wise $\gamma, \beta$ apply the same transform across all tokens; the per-token adaptation is what \cref{eq:dyt-linear} loses.

The above conclusions are drawn within the ViT-B/16 and SegFormer-B0 scale. Larger encoders may exhibit different per-token feature scales and different depth-dependent saturation profiles than \cref{fig:saturation} shows, so the pattern reported here should not be extrapolated upward without retesting. We also do not claim that DyT is intrinsically weak: the original paper shows strong from-initialization results across many settings. Within the post-hoc setting, our results identify a baseline that several follow-up directions can be measured against. Compute scaling is the most direct: the original DyT evaluation runs roughly an order of magnitude longer than our fine-tunes, so a longer schedule, especially on segmentation, would test whether the gap we report is fundamental or budget-bounded. Within the same budget, several adaptations are natural: site-selective replacement that excludes the worst sites (the final pre-classifier LN, where saturation peaks); activation-matching initialization that calibrates $\alpha, \gamma, \beta$ to the pretrained LayerNorm's input-output statistics on the first batches of fine-tuning; and DyT variants that restore some form of per-token scaling, directly addressing the candidate explanation above. Broader testing on Swin, MAE-pretrained ViT, and dense-prediction backbones beyond MiT-B0 would clarify how general the classification-versus-segmentation asymmetry is.

\subsection{Ethical considerations}
\label{subsec:ethics}

This work introduces no dataset, no human-subject data, and no deployed model. Image classification and semantic segmentation are nevertheless used in autonomous driving, medical imaging, surveillance, and remote sensing, where aggregate metrics can mask failures on rare classes and underrepresented environments, and an unreliable backbone can be misused when downstream decisions inherit those failures unchecked. Per-class IoU and per-class accuracy should accompany aggregate numbers before any DyT-substituted backbone is deployed, and a DyT replacement should not be adopted on from-initialization evidence alone without matched-accuracy validation against the LayerNorm baseline on the target dataset.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Dynamic Tanh was validated as a normalization replacement when present from the start of training. This paper tests the more practical post-hoc setting: replacing LayerNorm inside a pretrained vision encoder and relying on downstream fine-tuning to recover performance. Across ViT-B/16 classification and SegFormer-B0 segmentation, full replacement is not competitive with the LayerNorm baseline. Affine preservation substantially improves classification over DyT default, but it still leaves a large accuracy gap; in segmentation, the same adaptation does not help.

The main contribution is a bounded negative result with diagnostics. Post-hoc DyT failure is not a single phenomenon: ViT DyT default failure is consistent with tanh saturation, while SegFormer failure occurs despite low saturation and points toward sensitivity to the loss of per-token mean and variance. Follow-up directions, from compute scaling to site-selective replacement and activation-matching initialization, are detailed in \cref{sec:discussion}.
